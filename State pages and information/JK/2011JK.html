Spotted Deer

    Scientific name: Axis axis
    Status: Least Concern(but population decreasing)
    Weight: 36 kg (Adult)
    Size: About 35 inches
    Population: Fewer than 2300
    Habitat: They prefer dense deciduous forests, semi-evergreen forests, and open grasslands. They also prefer heavy forest cover for shade.
    Diet: Grazers as well as browsers, the chital mainly feed on grasses throughout the year. They prefer young shoots, in the absence of which, tall and coarse grasses are nibbled off at the tips.

Spotted Deer

The chital is listed by the IUCN as being of least concern "because it occurs over a very wide range within which there are many large populations".


Hunting for the deer's meat has caused substantial declines and local extinctions. The axis deer is protected under Schedule III of the Indian Wildlife Protection Act (1972) and under the Wildlife (Preservation) (Amendment) Act, 1974 of Bangladesh.

Tibetan Gazelle

Scientific name: Procapra picticaudata
Status: Near Threatened(Population decreasing)
Weight: 23 kg (Adult)
Size: 98 cm (Adult)
Population Density: 2.8 individuals per sq km to less than 0.1
Habitat: Alpine meadow and high elevation steppe are the primary habitats of the goa. It is scattered widely across its range, being present in numerous small herds spread wide apart; estimates of, depending on the local environment.
Range: The goa is native to the Tibetan plateau, and is widespread throughout the region, inhabiting terrain between 3,000 and 5,750 m (9,840 and 18,860 ft) in elevation.


×

Tibetan Gazelle

The population in Ladakh, less than 100 individuals, continues to decline. Within Ladakh, its distribution was spread as far west as the Tsokar Basin in the beginning of the 20th century, but today is confined to the Hanle Valley and the neighbouring areas, such as Chumur.


Presently gazelles are suffering not only from poor pasture conditions, but also from problems associated with small populations such as lack of genetic diversity in the population, which makes them less resistant to diseases.

Wild Yak

Scientific name: Bos mutus
Status: Rediscovered
Weight: 350 – 580 kg
Size: 2.5–3.3 m (In the wild, Without Tail)
Population: Around 26,221(in Jammu and Kashmir)
Habitat: Today, wild yaks are found primarily in northern Tibet and western Qinghai, with some populations extending into the southernmost parts of Xinjiang, and into Ladakh in India.
Diet: The diet of wild yaks consists largely of grasses and sedges, such as Carex, Stipa, and Kobresia.


×

Wild Yak

The wild yak is experiencing threats applied by several sources. Poaching, including commercial poaching, has remained the most serious threat; males are particularly impacted because of their more solitary habits.


Recent protection from poaching particularly appears to have stabilized or even increased population sizes in several areas, leading to the IUCN downlisting in 2008. In both China and India, the species is officially protected; in China it is present in a number of large nature reserves.

Black Necked Crane

Scientific name: Grus nigricollis
Status: Vulnerable (Population decreasing)
Weight: 5.5 kg (12 lbs)
Size: 139 cm long
Wingspan: 235 cm
Population: Between 8800 and 11000
Habitat: The black-necked crane summers mainly in the high altitude Tibetan Plateau.
Diet: Black-necked cranes feed on a variety of foods, including plant roots, tubers, earthworms, grasshoppers, snails, shrimp, small fish, frogs, lizards, beetles, flies, small rodents, and other small vertebrates and invertebrates.


×

Black Necked Crane

The Black-necked Crane (Grus nigricollis) is a medium-sized crane in Asia that breeds on the Tibetan Plateau and remote parts of India and Bhutan.


A festival in Bhutan celebrates the bird while the Indian union territory of Ladakh has designated it as the state bird.
